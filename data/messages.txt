Referee (2)

The article compares the social network of characters in ten books that are divided into  biographical, legendary and fictional texts. The main goal of the article, as stated in the introduction, is to find indexes that are able to differentiate or classify pure fiction, legendary accounts (that may have some historical traces) and historical biography.

The authors begin by analyzing global indexes, that are listed in Table 1. Surprisingly the data is just shown in the table (it is not cited in the text), and there is no comment or discussion on the main text on the values obtained. There are obvious mistakes in the calculations, since for example the clustering coefficient between the characters in the book "The Hobbit" is 0.000.  This is not possible since it is well known that the main character Bilbo Baggins shares his adventures with the wizard Gandalf and thirteen Dwarves.

Also for James Gleick's Isaac Newton the clustering coefficient presented in the table is 0.000.

The authors find that global measures do not show trends that allow them to separate the different genres, and they state that the Hapax legomena index allows them to separate fiction from historical works. If this was the case one would expect at least a clustering of the books in different categories. However this is not the case:  the index presents a continuum of values. To obtain a classification the authors divide the range into hand picked intervals. The criteria is quite weak given a range of values between 0.277 and 0.891. For example Tolkien (a biography) with a value of 0.512 is classified between  Luke Gospels (0.671) and Pythagoras(0.512), that are both regarded as Legendary accounts. This does not appear as a clear classification to me. Even more when Newton, the other biography, has the largest index (0.891) quite far from these values.

The same problem holds for the lower classification. Arthur (0.481, Fiction), has a value very close to Pythagoras (0.512, Legend) and Tolkien (0.512 Biography). While Hobbit also classified as Fiction has the lowest value (0.277).

I believe that even when the authors could find an index that shows some degree of clustering they should consider a larger database. Generalizing a classification based on such a small number of examples seems a  bold statement.

I believe that the article should be rejected. The authors should extend their database, revise their calculations and proof read the article with care before considering a resubmission to another journal.

\\
  We compare the social character networks of biographical, legendary and
fictional texts, in search of statistical marks of historical information. We
examine the frequency of character appearance and find a Zipf Law that does not
depend on the literary genera and historical content. We also examine global
and local complex networks indexes, in particular, correlation plots between
the recently introduced Lobby (or Hirsh $H(1)$) index and Degree, Betweenness
and Closeness centralities. We also found no relevant differences in the books
for these network indexes. We discovered, however, that a very simple index
based in the Hapax Legomena phenomenon (names cited a single time along the
text) that seems to have the potential of separating pure fiction from
legendary and biographical texts.
